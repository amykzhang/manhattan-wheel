The Influence of Genetics on BMI

Differences in our genetic code play a role in growth and risk of obesity during childhood. In this data story, we will look at the effect of genetic variation on Body Mass Index (BMI) in children.

What are SNPs?


Tiny differences in our genetic code are common and happen naturally among people. These differences can affect our physical traits, such as our height and weight, or how our body functions. They can even affect our chances of getting certain diseases.

These differences could be as small as a change in a single letter in our DNA. We refer to these changes as single nucleotide polymorphisms, long for SNPs (pronounced "snips").

Finding Important SNPs


Researchers study these genetic variants or SNPs to understand how they are connected to certain traits or diseases.

Take childhood obesity, for example. Scientists suspect that common genetic variations might affect a child's height, weight, and body mass index (BMI) as they grow.

To investigate this idea, scientists perform an analysis known as genome-wide association studies (GWAS). These studies investigate how the millions of SNPs that shape who we are associate with BMI. This research identifies SNPs that are most likely to influence BMI at a certain age during childhood.

Let's look at data from a GWAS study on childhood BMI in Norway. Specifically, data collected in early childhood, when the children were eight months old.

Each dot on this wheel represents a SNP in one of 23 chromosomes. The further the dot is from the center, the more likely it is to influence BMI at eight months old.

We've circled SNPs that scientists have found to significantly influence BMI at any point from birth to 8 years of age.

Let's take a closer look at this SNP and its effect on BMI.

We can plot this SNP's effect on BMI over time. We see this SNP has a small but consistent effect on increasing BMI at the beginning of childhood.

When plotting the effect of a SNP, we include error bars to show how confident we are about our estimation of the effect.

The closer the error bars are to the dot, the more precise and confident we are about the SNP's effect on BMI during childhood.

So how does this SNP increase BMI?

This genetic variation occurs in the leptin receptor (LEPR ) gene. This is one of the genes responsible for suppressing hunger and regulating weight.

Changing the abundance or activity of this gene alters the body's energy balance, which changes the weight and BMI of babies. This effect quickly disappears when the child begins to grow.
